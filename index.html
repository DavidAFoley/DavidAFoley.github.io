Registration?

There is much talk about registration at the moment. This proposal has encountered a mixed reception with drone fliers: commercial users are already subject to a strict regime with the requirement for a PfCO from the CAA. Recreational users are currently neither have to register nor to pass any awareness/competency test. Many like it that way. I'm a fan of registration. It won't solve the problem of renegade drone users, but it will help provide some security about the deployment and use of drones. Like many, I have good ideas about how I would like a registration system to work.

Some people see registration as either an infringement of their freedoms or an example of an overbearing state. I don't see it as either. Flying a drone has the potential to be either dangerous or intrusive, if not carried out properly. The things you need to do to make your flight as safe as can reasonably be expected are not onerous. They do require more though, than simply getting the drone out of the box, charging the battery and having a go. Registration is a reasonable response from a society that wants people to enjoy drones responsibly.

Some people wonder if registration will have any effect on improving safety. Surely, human nature is such that those who would fly safely anyway will dutifully register their drones and those who would show little concern for others will carry on doing so and won't register. I think it's true that there will be some people who won't register and that they may well be the people who are flying with poor airmanship. It strikes me that the majority of people would register, if it was required, and that this will help them understand their responsibilities and adhere to them. It also seems like it would make it easier for retailers if they knew there was one place to direct people.

Whilst I think that drone registration is a good idea, there are currently very few details about how it will operate. Here are a few suggestions of mine:

  • Make the cost proportionate - don't make registration seem disportionately expensive compared to the cost of the hobby (taking into account the cost of the drone and subsequent equipment). Presumably the scheme will have to cover its costs - so don't make it an expensive delivery model. Registration by app downloaded to the owner's smartphone and enabled using the barcode on the manufacturer's packaging sounds like a low cost way to go. Making it disproportionately expensive would surely discourage those you would most want to take part in the scheme.
  • Strong penalties - for people flying unregistered drones there should be a pretty severe penalty. Drone confiscation and destruction seems like a good place to start.
  • Provide a quick and responsive service - make registration quick and easy. If people have to post something off and wait weeks to get a reply, uptake will be low.

My Drone

Across much of photo/videography practitioners like to talk about equipment. Aerial photography is no different!

I fly the DJI Phantom 3 Standard. The choice of drones available to buy now is incredible and increasing all the time. There are multiple manufacturers that are credible and attractive. DJI is the largest of the consumer drone manufacturers, and perhaps not surprisingly, many people's route into aerial photography.

You can spend a lot (really, a lot) of money on a drone. The DJI Phantom 3 Standard is a good entry point, for a few hundred pounds you can be up and flying with a stable platform and reasonable camera and able to access most of the functionality that will enable you to capture stunning aerial shots. In terms of practicality, reliability and value for money, it's pretty hard to beat.

It's a standard quad: four propellers powered by four brushless motors. The camera shoots 2.7k video footage and 12MP stills. It uses the DJI Phantom 3 LiPo battery for power, which should be good for 15-20 mins of flight depending on conditions and at what reserve (I set my reminder to return home at 30%) you bring the drone home. The remote is comfortable to hold and has a capacious battery that easily lasts five or more flights. Its range is easily sufficient for flights within VLOS (visual line of sight). The DJI go app works seamlessly with the drone and gives you FPV as well as flight modes like course hold, follow me, point of interest and waypoints. The remote controls the gimbal as well as the drone so you can pan up and down remotely.

One day I will upgrade, but I will always have an affinity to the DJI phantom 3 - it's an amazingly capable drone at a very obtainable price.

Useful resources: great apps

As with most parts of our life technology is opening up new opportunities and enabling us to pursue our interest in ever different ways. Aerial photography is no different, in fact, because it is an emerging technology itself, you could well expect it to be at the leading edge of exploring what technology can help you do. I list here a few of the resources that I have found to be worth investing time in. This is a far from comprehensive list of what is out there, just some of my favourites.

  1. DJI Go

    There are a variety of pilot interface apps out there, but the one produced by DJI (NB: for use with DJI drones) is a really good one. I've found it reliable and also intuitive. The layout is uncluttered and you get a good, clear feed from the onboard camera.

  2. NATS Drone Assist

    Well done to NATS for producing such a good app. This helps with your pre-flight preparation, enabling you to check the airspace for suitability to fly, and acting as a very useful prompt of the drone code. Added is the ability to register your flight so that other users can see in which vicinity you are operating (this only works if you register your flight - please do, it helps everyone).

  3. Phantom Pilots

    This is not an app but an online community. You can post questions, answer them if you have the knowledge and see previous threads. It is a mine of information and there are some very knowledgeable pilots as well as some interesting threads on this site.

  4. Dark Sky

    A really good weather app. Giving great local level data and in a very useable format. I've found it to be one of the most accurate forecasts I've used. You can also contribute weather observations of your own to help them validate their forecasts.

  5. Drone Deploy

    Useful if you want to take a series of photos of a site in order to create some sort of map. Has both free and paid for versions. Remember, if you're making any sort of return on your work you need a PfCO from the CAA.

A word about safety

I don't mean to burst anyone's bubble about the excitement of flying a drone but it is worth talking about safe flying. It's a topic I'm passionate about and will come back to quite regularly. That's not to mean I'm a killjoy, you won't find safety requirements so proscriptive that it is impossible to fly here, but I do think about safety first and foremost whenever I'm thinking about flying or am flying.

This is a responsibility beholden on all drone pilots. You may be flying for recreational rather than commercial reasons (and by the way, if you're selling your creations you need to have a PfCO - Permission for Commercial Operation - from the Civil Aviation Authority) but you still owe a duty of care to everyone else. You may also think this is all overblown. After all, your drone is pretty light, mainly plastic and seems very dependable. All of that is true, but when you're flying it at any height, or any speed, and something goes wrong - that small plastic device can cause serious injury. You don't have to look far on the BBC News webpage to find some horror stories about what can go wrong.

The good news is, you can easily take practical steps to minimise the risk. Firstly, familiarise yourself with the drone code. You can find this here.

So:

  • Not in restricted zones (e.g. airfields, prisons)
  • No more than 400ft high (less likely to contact aircraft)
  • In visual line of sight (so you can see where it's going and what is all around it)
  • 50m away from individuals and 150m from crowds and built up areas

Make sure you look after your craft. Simple measures like keeping it clean, looking for cracks or dents, ensuring everything is screwed up tight, changing propellers if you scratch or dent them. These can make a real difference.

Prepare before you fly. What is the weather forecast? Can your craft cope with the conditions? Are you equipped for the conditions so that you're not distracted? Have someone with you, who can help be your eyes and ears whilst you are flying.

It's not that hard, but we all need to do it. There's more than this that we can do as drone pilots, but start with remembering to do all of these. Otherwise, the best case scenario is society will run out of patience with us as a group. The worst case scenario is you will injure yourself or someone else. Neither of which bear thinking about. If you have any doubts about the safety of your flight, then there is no doubt: don't fly.

Be safe. Then you really can enjoy yourself and get all this wonderful hobby has to offer.

Aerial photography: A love affair

Have you ever found something so exhilerating and stimulating that you just wanted to do it again, and again and again? That was how I felt the first time I flew a drone. And the next, and the the next, and still feel several years and hundreds of flights later. There is something about drone flying that feels special and that I've never tired of.

It's hard to encapsulate in words exactly what it is that makes drone flying so extraordinary, but I think there are three aspects that particularly stand out.

Firstly, although you never leave the ground yourself the feel of piloting a craft that is flying, especially with the advent of First Person View (FPV) - i.e. the ability to see a view on your screen routed directly from the craft) - is one of flying. Flying is special, very special. I doubt many people, the world over, have failed to imagine what it would be like to fly, and piloting a drone gives you some of that sensation.

Secondly, flying a drone allows you to see the world from an entirely new perspective. Places you thought you knew well, and that you have seen hundreds or thousands of time, suddenly reveal new and enticing sides. You can explore areas you know intimately as if for the first time again. Patterns that you can't recognise from ground level suddenly become obvious from above. Exploration is writ large in drone flying.

Thirdly, like all photography, you are painting with light. With a drone you can put your camera in places that allow you to paint with light that you would otherwise not be able to access. Angles change, panoramas appear and light dapples places you couldn't otherwise see. It's for some reason that people refer to drones as tripods in the sky.

I leave you with a photo I took of Cleadon Hills in the North East of England, a place I have walked in many times, but this is a photo I took with a drone that captures the place better than any other I have taken.
